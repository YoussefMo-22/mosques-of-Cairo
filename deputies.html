The History and Features of the Deputies age:

After the Arab Islamic conquest, the representatives of Egypt ruled after the Islamic conquest and ruled it on behalf of the caliph in the capital of the Islamic state in Medina. This period lasted from the Islamic conquest in the year 20 AH until 254 AH when the Tulunid state began. This period was called the age of deputies, the "era of loyalty". During this period the Islamic state ruled by the Rightly Guided Caliphs was as follows: - The rule of Omar Ibn Al-Khattab 13-23 AH. - The rule of Othman bin Affan 23-35 AH. - Ali bin Abi Talib era 35-40 AH. After the end of the Rashidun rule, the Umayyad state was The rule of the Islamic State began in the year 40 AH until its fall In the year 132 AH. The Umayyad state was defeated by the Abbasid armies in 132 AH, and the Abbasid state ruled the Islamic world. . During the Abbasid state: Egypt was ruled by an ambitious prince on behalf of his deputy in the year 254 AH. This prince started to take real steps to make Egypt an independent city. He succeeded in achieving his goal of declaring a new era of Egyptian history. As soon as the Tulunid state began, Egypt entered a new era called the period of the independent states, the era of the resigned Diwali.

The History of the Tulunid state [254 - 290 AH]

The origins of the Tulunid family are Turkish. That this state was attributed to Toulon, who was a Turkish slave of Noah Al-Samani, the governor of Bukhara. The ruler of Bukhara provided a number of slaves - Including Toulon - as a gift to the Abbasid Mamun Succession. Tulun was promoted to Amir and his son Ahmed was born in Baghdad in 220 AH. Ahmed Ibn Tulun received a good education next to the throne of the Abbasid caliph. Ibn Tulun extracted his colleagues from the Mamluks until he earned the trust of the Abbasid caliph. After the death of his father, Tulun, the Abbasid caliph assumed all the positions that had belonged to his father. . In the year 254 AH. The Abbasid caliph al-Mu'tazz appointed the Turkish leader Qubak as deputy for Egypt, but he preferred to remain next to the throne of the caliphate. He chose Ibn Tulun to be the ruler of Egypt one day on his behalf and supported him with a great army which entered Egypt in the same year. Ibn Tulun began to rule Egypt and overcame all the obstacles that confronted him. Obstacles that faced Ibn Tulun The first obstacle that Ibn Tulun faced in Egypt was the postal worker who directed his loyalty to the caliph and not to the caliph. ruler of Egypt. The postal worker during this period was called Shakir, and he was a slave to Umm Al-Moataz. Skair realized the danger of Ibn Tulun early on Abbasid caliphate and send a message to the caliph to warn for him. Ibn Tulun acquired the message and took real steps to remove the postal worker from his position, and he succeeded in that In his own net after the death of Al-Moataz. . By deposing Shakir from his position, Ibn Tulun keeps his secrets inside his city. The second obstacle represented in the taxes worker 'amil al-Kharaj who was called ibn al- Mudabbir, that the caliphate used to separate the kharaj affairs on the political rule. The kharaj worker imposed many taxes on the people, so the relationship between him, the ruler and the people was deteriorated. Ibn Tulun managed to isolate ibn al-Mudabbir from his position. The following events served Ibn Tulun that Bakbak was dead and his position moved to Yargog who was a father-in-law of Ibn Tulun.

Egypt under the Ikhshidid state [323 - 358 AH]

After the fall of the Tulunid state in 292 AH. Egypt became again following the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad until the year 323 AH when the Abbasid caliph appointed the Amir Muhammad ibn Tughj as a ruler on it. The origin of this family was Turkish from Firghnah. The grand father of Muhammad ibn Tughj -Jiff- came from Firghanah to Samarra in reign of the caliph al-Mo'tasim. After the death of jiff, his son Tughj connected with Ibn Tulun and became one of his greatest leaders. Egypt under the Ikhshidid state Tughj passed away in 294 AH and his son Mohamed inherited the position of his father until he appointed as a ruler on Egypt by the order of al-Qahir in 321 AH and continued for only 32 days. Mohmed ibn Tughj again appointed as a ruler over Egypt in 323 AH by the order of the caliph al-Radi, this time, his rule continued for 11 years and he asked the caliph to donate him the nickname al-Ikhsid which means in Firghanah malik al-miluk. Al-Ikhside takes the same steps of the Tulunids as he made Egypt an independent state. Egypt under the Ikhshidid state Firstly, al-Ikhshid assumed only the political affairs whereas the kharaj was in the hand of the abbasid vizier Ga'far ibn al-Furat who spend one year in Egypt during the rule of al-Ikhshid and then traveled to Baghdad. After that al-Ikhshid gathered the political and the kharaj affairs to begin his real independence from the caliphate. The relationship between al-Ikhshid and the caliphate During the rule of al-Ikhshid, the Abbasid caliphate began to weaken as the caliphs forced to find a new position in the caliphate called amir al-Umara who had the real power in the caliphate. In 324 AH, ibn Ra'iq assumed this position, he asked al-Ikhshid to send more money to the caliphate which strained the relation between them. Ibn Ra'iq attempted to extract the Levant from al-Ikhshid but al-Ikhshid defeated his army and fixed his rule in Egypt and the Levant.
