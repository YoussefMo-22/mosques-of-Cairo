Al-Refai Mosque

History

The al-Rifa'i Mosque, located in Salah al-Din Square (or Maydan al-Qal'a, Citadel Square), was built in the 19th century and resembles the neighboring mosque of al-Sultan Ḥassan in the 14th century. The mosque is named after the tomb of Ali, the grandson of Imam Ahmad al-Rifay, known as 'Abu Sheybaq'. When the mosque was completed, it was directly attributed to Ahmad al-Rifay, who founded the Rifai Sufi Order. Although he was never buried here, the mosque hosts a joyful annual Sufi celebration to mark his birth. The original structure was a Fatimid mosque that was converted into a shrine to Ali Abu Sheybaq. finally, Khedive Ismail's mother, her Hoshiyar Hanim, commissioned the design of the current mosque and commissioned architect Hussein Pasha Fahmi to build it. Part of the plan included a mausoleum for Muhammad Ali's family as part of the extension, which was constructed with building materials imported from Europe, including Italian marble. Construction was suspended for years until it was completed in 1330 AD in the area of ​​Kedib Abbashelmi his II. Part of the mosque is dedicated to prayer, while another is reserved for the family of Muhammad, his Ali, and his Pasha, who ruled from the early 19th century until his 1953 when the modern Egyptian Republic was born. It has been. Kedib Ismail and his mother Hoshiyar Kadin, as well as the last two rulers of the Muhammad Ali dynasty, King Fuad I and King Farouk, are buried here, all in elaborate tombs. The mosque contains the burial chamber of Muhammad Reza Pahlavi (d. 1400 AH/1980 AD), the last Shah of Iran.

Architecture

The architectural design of the Al Rifai Mosque is as interesting as its architectural history. Visitors are amazed by the intricate decoration of the exterior walls and the heavy pillars of the outer gate. The minaret stands out for its beauty and grace. In addition, cement was used in the construction of a mosque for the first time in an Islamic building in Egypt, indicating the transition to modern times. This monument represents a turning point in Egypt's cultural and political history. It deals with straight boulevards and squares, two aspects of European urban planning introduced during the reign of Muhammad Ali and his successors, who sought to transform Egypt's traditional society into a cosmopolitan society. Conceived as a stand-alone monument, Al-Rifay Mosque responds to its location by presenting four fully articulated façades along with ornate domes and Mamluk-style minarets. The mosque is an architectural expression of the dynasty's renewed quest for legitimacy through the use of national symbols. The mosque's association with Mamluk heritage is evident in its admirable imitation of the adjacent Sultan Hassan his mosque in mass, material use, color and detail. Its integration into the context of the popular city of Zawiya is a clear sign of the dynasty's desire to appeal to much-needed popular support in the face of increasing British rule.
